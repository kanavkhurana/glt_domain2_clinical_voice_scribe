These protocols are synthetic teaching documents aligned to ICMR Clinical Practice Guidelines structure. They are NOT official ICMR documents — they are constructed for the AceAI bootcamp prototype.

## PROTOCOL 1: ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROME (ACS) — PRIMARY CARE TRIAGE

### When to suspect
- Chest pain/heaviness/tightness with or without radiation to left arm, jaw, back
- Shortness of breath with exertion in patients >40 years
- New onset diaphoresis (sweating) with chest discomfort
- **HIGH RISK GROUPS:** Diabetics (may present atypically — epigastric pain, fatigue, dyspnea without chest pain), hypertensives, smokers, family history of premature CAD

### Red flags — immediate action required
- Chest pain + sweating + breathlessness (classic triad)
- Chest pain in known diabetic (even if atypical)
- New onset arrhythmia with hemodynamic instability
- Syncope with chest pain

### Primary care protocol
1. **Immediate:** ECG within 10 minutes of presentation
2. **Aspirin 325mg** chewable stat (unless allergy documented)
3. **Sublingual Nitroglycerin** 0.4mg if BP >90 systolic (up to 3 doses, 5 min apart)
4. **Oxygen** if SpO2 <94%
5. **Do NOT delay referral** — call ambulance/transfer to nearest cath lab facility
6. **Troponin** if available; do not wait for results before transfer

### Documentation requirements
- Time of symptom onset (critical for thrombolysis/PCI window)
- Time of first medical contact
- ECG findings
- Medications administered with timestamps

### What the AI should flag
- Any chest pain + diabetes → RED FLAG
- Any chest pain + age >50 + hypertension → RED FLAG
- Exertional dyspnea + sweating → RED FLAG even without chest pain
- Silent MI presentation in diabetics → HIGH ALERT

## PROTOCOL 2: TYPE 2 DIABETES MELLITUS — OUTPATIENT MANAGEMENT

### Diagnostic criteria (ICMR/WHO)
- Fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL (on two occasions)
- 2-hour post-prandial glucose ≥200 mg/dL (OGTT)
- HbA1c ≥6.5%
- Random glucose ≥200 mg/dL with symptoms

### Treatment targets
| Parameter | Target |
|---|---|
| HbA1c | <7.0% (individualise: <6.5% if young, no complications; <8.0% if elderly, comorbid) |
| Fasting glucose | 80-130 mg/dL |
| Post-prandial glucose | <180 mg/dL |
| BP | <130/80 mmHg |
| LDL cholesterol | <100 mg/dL (<70 if established CVD) |

### First-line therapy
- **Metformin** starting dose 500mg OD with meals, titrate to 1000mg BD over 4-8 weeks
- Contraindications: eGFR <30, active liver disease, acute illness, peri-operative period

### When to add second agent
- HbA1c >7.5% on maximised Metformin after 3 months
- Options: Sulfonylurea (Glimepiride), DPP-4 inhibitor (Sitagliptin), SGLT2 inhibitor (Dapagliflozin)

### Monitoring schedule
- HbA1c every 3 months until target reached, then every 6 months
- Renal function (eGFR, urine ACR) annually
- Lipid profile annually
- Eye examination annually (dilated fundoscopy)
- Foot examination every visit

### What the AI should flag
- Fasting glucose >250 or HbA1c >10% → urgent review
- eGFR <30 on Metformin → contraindication alert
- Missed eye/foot/renal screening → documentation gap

## PROTOCOL 3: HYPERTENSION — DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT

### Diagnosis (ICMR/ISH guidelines)
- Office BP ≥140/90 mmHg on two separate occasions
- Home BP ≥135/85 mmHg (average of 7-day monitoring)
- White coat hypertension: consider ABPM

### Classification
| Category | Systolic | Diastolic |
|---|---|---|
| Normal | <120 | <80 |
| Elevated | 120-129 | <80 |
| Stage 1 | 130-139 | 80-89 |
| Stage 2 | ≥140 | ≥90 |
| Hypertensive crisis | >180 | >120 |

### First-line therapy (Indian context)
- ACE inhibitor (Enalapril, Ramipril) OR ARB (Telmisartan, Losartan)
- Calcium channel blocker (Amlodipine) — preferred in elderly, isolated systolic HTN
- Thiazide diuretic (Hydrochlorothiazide) — add-on

### Drug interactions to flag
- ACE inhibitor + ARB: DO NOT combine (increased renal risk)
- ACE inhibitor/ARB + potassium-sparing diuretic: hyperkalemia risk
- NSAIDs + any antihypertensive: NSAIDs reduce efficacy
- NSAIDs + ACE/ARB + diuretic: "triple whammy" — acute kidney injury risk

### What the AI should flag
- BP >180/120 → hypertensive crisis, immediate assessment
- New headache + BP >160 → rule out secondary causes
- NSAIDs prescribed alongside antihypertensives → drug interaction alert

## PROTOCOL 4: PEDIATRIC ACUTE GASTROENTERITIS — DEHYDRATION MANAGEMENT

### Dehydration assessment (WHO/ICMR)

| Sign | No dehydration | Some dehydration | Severe dehydration |
|---|---|---|---|
| General condition | Well, alert | Restless, irritable | Lethargic, unconscious |
| Eyes | Normal | Sunken | Very sunken |
| Tears | Present | Absent | Absent |
| Mouth/tongue | Moist | Dry | Very dry |
| Skin pinch | Goes back quickly | Goes back slowly | Goes back very slowly |
| Urine output | Normal | Reduced | None for >6 hours |

### Treatment by severity
**No dehydration (Plan A):**
- ORS after each loose stool: <2 years: 50-100ml; 2-10 years: 100-200ml; >10 years: as much as wanted
- Continue breastfeeding/feeding
- Zinc: <6 months: 10mg/day x 14 days; ≥6 months: 20mg/day x 14 days

**Some dehydration (Plan B):**
- ORS: 75ml/kg over 4 hours
- Reassess every 1-2 hours
- If not improving → Plan C

**Severe dehydration (Plan C):**
- IV Ringer's Lactate or Normal Saline immediately
- 30ml/kg in first 30 min (infants) or first 1 hour (children >1 year)
- Then 70ml/kg over next 5 hours (infants) or 2.5 hours (children)
- Hospital admission mandatory

### Red flags — immediate hospital referral
- Weight loss >10% of body weight
- No urine output for 6+ hours
- Persistent vomiting (cannot keep ORS down)
- Bloody diarrhea
- High fever (>39°C) with dehydration
- Altered consciousness

### What the AI should flag
- Weight loss calculation from previous visit → auto-calculate % dehydration
- No urine for 6+ hours in child → SEVERE dehydration alert
- Blood in stool → possible dysentery, antibiotic consideration

## PROTOCOL 5: ASTHMA — ACUTE EXACERBATION MANAGEMENT

### Severity assessment

| Parameter | Mild | Moderate | Severe | Life-threatening |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Talks in | Sentences | Phrases | Words | Cannot speak |
| SpO2 | >95% | 92-95% | <92% | <90% |
| Peak flow | >75% predicted | 50-75% | 25-50% | <25% |
| Respiratory rate (adult) | <25 | 25-30 | >30 | Bradypnea |
| Heart rate | <110 | 110-120 | >120 | Bradycardia |
| Accessory muscles | No | Yes | Yes | Paradoxical movement |

### Step-up treatment
**Step 1 (Mild):** Salbutamol MDI 4-8 puffs via spacer, repeat Q20min x 3
**Step 2 (Moderate):** Add Ipratropium 4 puffs. Oral Prednisolone 40mg (adult) / 1-2mg/kg (child)
**Step 3 (Severe):** Continuous nebulisation. IV Magnesium sulphate 2g over 20 min. Prepare for ICU.
**Step 4 (Life-threatening):** Intubation and mechanical ventilation. IV Salbutamol. ICU transfer.

### Pediatric SpO2 thresholds
- SpO2 >95%: manage at home with follow-up
- SpO2 92-95%: observe 1-2 hours post-treatment; discharge if responding
- SpO2 <92%: hospital admission
- SpO2 <90%: ICU/HDU

### What the AI should flag
- SpO2 <92% in any asthma patient → SEVERE exacerbation
- SpO2 <95% in child → borderline for admission
- Patient on preventer inhaler who stopped → likely trigger for exacerbation
- Silent chest (no wheeze in severe distress) → LIFE-THREATENING

## PROTOCOL 6: PREGNANCY — FIRST TRIMESTER CARE

### First visit investigations (per ICMR ANC guidelines)
- Hemoglobin, blood group + Rh
- Random blood sugar (GDM screening if high risk)
- TSH (mandatory — hypothyroidism common in India)
- Urine routine + culture
- VDRL/RPR
- HIV (with consent)
- Hepatitis B surface antigen
- Ultrasound: viability + dating (ideally 6-8 weeks)
- Rubella IgG (if no documented immunity)

### Red flags in first trimester
- Vaginal bleeding → threatened abortion, ectopic pregnancy
- Severe vomiting (>5 times/day, unable to keep fluids) → hyperemesis gravidarum
- Abdominal pain + bleeding + dizziness → ectopic pregnancy until proven otherwise
- BP >140/90 in first visit → chronic hypertension (pre-existing)

### Thyroid in pregnancy
- TSH target: <2.5 mIU/L in first trimester
- Existing hypothyroid patients: increase Levothyroxine dose by 30-50% as soon as pregnancy confirmed
- Recheck TSH every 4 weeks in first trimester

### Medications to avoid
- Retinoids (Isotretinoin, Adapalene) — teratogenic
- Warfarin — teratogenic in first trimester
- ACE inhibitors / ARBs — fetotoxic
- Methotrexate — abortifacient
- NSAIDs — avoid especially in third trimester

### What the AI should flag
- First trimester bleeding → ultrasound needed to rule out ectopic
- TSH >2.5 in pregnant patient → dose adjustment needed
- Any Category X drug in pregnant patient → CRITICAL alert

## PROTOCOL 7: COMMON DRUG INTERACTIONS — PRIMARY CARE

### Critical interactions (NEVER co-prescribe without specialist input)

| Drug A | Drug B | Risk | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warfarin | NSAIDs (any) | Major bleeding | CRITICAL |
| Warfarin | Aspirin (>100mg) | Major bleeding | CRITICAL |
| Warfarin | Tranexamic acid | Thromboembolism | CRITICAL |
| ACE inhibitor | Potassium supplement | Hyperkalemia → cardiac arrest | CRITICAL |
| Methotrexate | NSAIDs | Methotrexate toxicity | CRITICAL |
| MAO inhibitor | SSRI/SNRI | Serotonin syndrome | CRITICAL |

### Moderate interactions (monitor closely)

| Drug A | Drug B | Risk | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| NSAIDs | ACE/ARB | Reduced antihypertensive effect, renal risk | Monitor BP, renal function |
| NSAIDs | Diuretics | Reduced diuretic effect, renal risk | Monitor renal function |
| Metformin | Contrast dye | Lactic acidosis | Hold Metformin 48h pre/post |
| Corticosteroids | NSAIDs | GI bleeding | Add PPI cover |
| SSRIs | NSAIDs | GI bleeding (2-3x risk) | Add PPI cover |
| Statins | Macrolides | Myopathy/rhabdomyolysis | Use Azithromycin (safest macrolide) |

### What the AI should flag
- Any CRITICAL interaction → RED FLAG with alternative suggestion
- Any MODERATE interaction → WARNING with monitoring recommendation
- Multiple NSAIDs prescribed simultaneously → FLAG (common error)

## PROTOCOL 8: MENTAL HEALTH SCREENING — PRIMARY CARE

### When to screen
- Patient mentions sleep disturbance, fatigue, loss of interest lasting >2 weeks
- Chronic pain with no clear organic cause
- Frequent clinic visits with multiple somatic complaints
- Caregiver burnout (parents of sick children, elderly caregivers)

### PHQ-2 Quick Screen
1. Over the past 2 weeks, how often have you been bothered by little interest or pleasure in doing things?
2. Over the past 2 weeks, how often have you been bothered by feeling down, depressed, or hopeless?

Score ≥3 → proceed to PHQ-9

### Safety assessment triggers
If patient mentions:
- Suicidal thoughts → assess immediacy, plan, means
- Self-harm → assess recency, method, frequency
- Harm to others → assess target, plan
- Domestic violence → assess safety, offer resources

### What the AI should flag
- Keywords: "wanting to die", "no reason to live", "better off without me", "can't take it anymore" → IMMEDIATE SAFETY FLAG
- This flag must override all other processing — surface to physician immediately
- AI must NOT counsel, advise, or attempt to assess — flag to human only

## PROTOCOL 9: ANTIMICROBIAL STEWARDSHIP — PRIMARY CARE

### When antibiotics are NOT indicated
- Viral upper respiratory infection (common cold)
- Acute bronchitis in otherwise healthy adults
- Viral gastroenteritis (no blood, no fever >39°C, no severe dehydration)
- Non-specific sore throat without positive strep test

### When antibiotics ARE indicated
- Confirmed bacterial pneumonia (clinical/X-ray)
- Urinary tract infection (confirmed by symptoms + urine culture/dipstick)
- Streptococcal pharyngitis (positive rapid strep test or culture)
- Acute otitis media in children <2 years, or severe symptoms
- Cellulitis / skin abscess with systemic signs

### First-line antibiotic choices (ICMR AMR guidelines)
| Condition | First-line | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Uncomplicated UTI (women) | Nitrofurantoin 100mg BD | 5 days |
| Strep pharyngitis | Amoxicillin 500mg TDS | 10 days |
| Acute otitis media (peds) | Amoxicillin 80-90mg/kg/day | 7-10 days |
| Community-acquired pneumonia | Amoxicillin 500mg TDS or Azithromycin 500mg OD | 5-7 days |
| Cellulitis | Cephalexin 500mg QDS | 5-7 days |

### What the AI should flag
- Antibiotic prescribed for viral URI → stewardship alert
- Fluoroquinolone as first-line for UTI → not recommended per ICMR (resistance >30%)
- Duration >14 days for any community infection → review indication

## PROTOCOL 10: GERIATRIC CARE — FALL RISK AND POLYPHARMACY

### Fall risk assessment
High risk if ≥2 of:
- Age >65
- Previous fall in last 12 months
- Gait or balance disorder
- Visual impairment
- Cognitive impairment
- ≥4 medications (polypharmacy)
- Postural hypotension
- Environmental hazards at home

### Medications that increase fall risk
- Benzodiazepines and sedatives
- Antihypertensives (especially alpha-blockers, diuretics)
- Opioids
- Anticholinergics
- Antidepressants (SSRIs and tricyclics)
- Antipsychotics

### Post-fall protocol
1. Assess for fracture (hip, wrist, spine — most common)
2. Check for head injury (even if patient denies — elderly may not report)
3. Lying-standing BP (postural hypotension is the most common reversible cause)
4. Medication review — can any fall-risk medication be stopped or reduced?
5. ECG — rule out arrhythmia
6. Blood glucose — hypoglycemia as cause
7. Bone density screening if fragility fracture

### What the AI should flag
- Patient >65 + fall + any fracture → osteoporosis screening needed
- Patient on ≥5 medications → polypharmacy review recommended
- Benzodiazepine use in elderly → fall risk alert
- Post-fall with dizziness → cardiac and postural workup needed
